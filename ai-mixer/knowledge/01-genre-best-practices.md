# Genre Mixing Best Practices: Garage, UK Garage, Drill, Trap

Compiled from producer interviews, engineer breakdowns, and tutorial content
by Attack Magazine, iZotope, Mixmag, Sound on Sound, BandLab, and others.
Citations inline. When suggestions in this assistant cite "01-genre" they're
referencing this file.

---

## Garage (classic UK 2-step / speed garage roots)

### DO's

- **130 BPM with 58-66% swing** on the 16th-note grid. MJ Cole references
  Logic's 66% (16th-note triplet) feel as canonical 2-step. (Attack Magazine,
  *Beat Dissected: Garage Shuffle*)
- **Layer two distinct kicks**: a snappy 909-based kick with a 707 transient
  layer, plus a longer 808-based kick used for contrast on alternating bars.
  Hard-quantize the kick on beat 1 and snares on 2-and-4; let hats/percussion
  drift slightly off-grid for groove.
- **16th-note tambourine/shaker as the shuffle engine.** Drummers don't play
  continuous 16ths; percussion fills that role with strong velocity variation.
- **Pitch snares slightly up** and shorten the amp-envelope release to keep
  them tight; layer a chopped funk-break sample on top of the electronic
  snare for character.
- **Bus-glue the drums** with parallel compression and gentle tape saturation
  to fuse the sampled-break and drum-machine layers.
- **Mono sub below ~100 Hz**, light sidechain on the sub from the kick:
  fastest attack, ~3-5 dB GR, taste-based release. (Attack Magazine,
  *Subby Garage Bass*)
- **Todd Edwards-style vocal chops**: short, granular, pitch-shifted hits
  used as both melodic and percussive elements. Slap delay or short plate.
- **Punchy 4x4 kicks need low-mid presence (200-400 Hz)** so they don't get
  swallowed by the sub. Reserve the bottom for the bassline.

### DON'T's

- **Don't over-quantize.** Feel is the genre. Snapping every hi-hat to the
  grid kills the swing.
- **Don't let claps sustain.** Long tails smear the off-beat backbeat —
  shorten release, preserve transient.
- **Don't pump heavy EDM-style sidechain.** Garage relies on the natural
  off-beat space between kick and bass, not visible ducking.
- **Don't pile EQ/compression on every track.** Classic garage records were
  comparatively lightly processed.
- **Don't run sub-bass in stereo.** Mono below 100-120 Hz or you lose punch
  on club PAs.
- **Don't make the drum pattern busy.** Garage is about negative space and
  the kick *not* playing on every beat.

## UK Garage / UKG (modern revival — Sammy Virji, Notion, Interplanetary Criminal era)

> Sammy Virji and Notion are the touchstones for this assistant's UKG
> suggestions. Virji defines the bouncy, broken-drum, FM-bass aesthetic.
> Notion (Mixmag's April 2026 cover) defines the high-energy, genre-spanning
> drum approach.

### DO's

- **Tempo cluster: 135-138 BPM.** Modern UKG (Sammy Virji, Interplanetary
  Criminal, Conducta, Main Phase, Notion) lives here. Pure 130 reads as
  classic garage.
- **Two-tier bass design.** Keep the main bass-stab dry and high-passed at
  ~100 Hz. Drop a separate dedicated sub-sample below for low-end weight.
  This stops sub buildup on the stab itself and creates contrast when the sub
  enters. (Attack Magazine, *Beat Dissected: Interplanetary Criminal*)
- **Light sidechain on the sub** keyed from the kick: fastest attack, release
  ~50-80 ms, target ~3-5 dB GR. Kick and bass are syncopated, not locked, so
  you only need a kiss of duck.
- **Prefer dynamic EQ over wideband sidechain** for kick/bass separation.
  Duck the bass's fundamental (60-70 Hz, tight Q, ~3 dB GR) so the mid-range
  growl stays present. (Music Guy Mixing, *Sidechain EQ*)
- **FM/Reese sub design** in the Virji lineage: Meld or Serum FM patches with
  controlled modulation and short percussive amp envelopes that rest on
  off-beats. (Attack Magazine, Sammy Virji tutorial)
- **Pitch-shifted vocal chops** with high-pass at ~200 Hz, short plate or
  slap delay. Keep them upfront and percussive — never bury in hall reverb.
- **Hi-hats: alternate two close hits to accentuate the swing.** Faster
  tempos = less hat swing; let percussion (shaker, ride) carry shuffle.
- **Drum-bus glue:** ~2 dB GR bus compression (slow attack, auto release) +
  tape/console saturation to unify chopped break + drum-machine layers.
- **Broken/bouncy drums (Virji signature):** layer a chopped break on top of
  programmed kick/snare. The break carries swing and human imperfection;
  the programmed layer carries impact. See `02-knock-warmth-3d-drums.md`
  for the layering technique.

### DON'T's

- **Don't sidechain the entire bass band heavily.** That's house/EDM; it
  kills the syncopated push-pull that makes UKG bounce.
- **Don't widen the sub.** Mono below ~120 Hz or it disappears in clubs.
- **Don't quantize hi-hats to a strict 16th grid.** Use DAW groove templates
  (e.g. SP1200 Swing-71 at 60%) on hats only.
- **Don't bury vocal chops in hall reverb.** Modern UKG keeps them upfront;
  short plate or slap, never hall.
- **Don't master too loud.** UKG sits closer to club-house loudness
  (-8 to -9 LUFS integrated). Preserve kick transients.
- **Don't double-up two subs in the same octave.** Pick one sub, let the
  bass-stab fill the mids.

## Drill (UK drill primary)

### DO's

- **Tempo 140-145 BPM, halftime feel.** Snares hit on **beat 3**, not 2 and
  4 — this is the single biggest tell that separates drill from trap.
- **Hi-hat pattern: grime-derived "two spaces, two spaces, one space"** on
  the 16th grid. Sprinkle 1/8T or 1/16T triplet rolls and 32nd flams for
  ornament. Avoid even 16th runs — that's trap territory.
- **Sliding mono 808 with portamento.** UK drill 808s slide between root and
  5th/octave on nearly every bar; the slide *is* the bassline. Use FL
  Studio's slide notes or mono+glide on Serum.
- **Subtle saturation/soft-clip on the 808** (FL Soft Clipper at -3 to -6 dB
  threshold, or Decapitator) to expose harmonics on phone speakers without
  losing pitch definition.
- **Layered snare = body (150-300 Hz) + clap with sharp transient (2-5 kHz).**
  UK drill specifically delays the clap a few ticks behind the snare for the
  "slap" feel. (BandLab, *How to Make a UK Drill Beat*)
- **High-pass snares/claps at 130-150 Hz** to clear the 808's low end.
- **Ghost snares at 15-25% velocity** create the rhythmic shuffle that
  distinguishes UK drill from NY drill's stiffer feel.
- **Cut low-mids (~250-400 Hz) on melody/strings** to leave room for 808 and
  kick fundamental. Boost hat top-end (8-12 kHz) for crisp bite.
- **Sparse arrangement.** Halftime melody + sliding 808 + snare on 3 is the
  whole skeleton. Leave 1-2 bars of just 808+kick before melody returns.
- **Vocal chain:** aggressive multi-stage compression (1176-style fast comp
  hitting 4-6 dB into a slower LA-2A-style hitting 2-3 dB) plus de-essing.

### DON'T's

- **Don't layer two subs on the 808.** Phasing and mud are guaranteed; one
  mono 808 + saturation handles both sub and harmonic content.
- **Don't widen the 808** with stereo plugins. Drill 808s are dead-mono.
- **Don't quantize hats too rigidly.** Micro-shifts and triplet flams are
  signature; perfectly gridded hats sound like trap.
- **Don't over-distort the 808.** UK drill 808s must retain melodic clarity
  since they often double the topline.
- **Don't put the snare on 2 and 4.** That's generic trap; drill = halftime
  snare on 3.
- **Don't use long hall reverbs on snares.** Short 1-1.5s plate/room only,
  otherwise the halftime snare loses impact.

### US drill notes
- **NY/Brooklyn drill** (808Melo, AXL Beats, Pop Smoke): straighter,
  less-shuffled hats; grime-derived basslines often even more bass-heavy.
- **Chicago drill** (Young Chop / Lil Reese era): ~140 BPM, more sustained
  808s, less sliding — closer to early trap.

## Trap (Metro Boomin / Southside / Wheezy / TM88 era)

### DO's

- **Tempo 130-150 BPM**, often felt halftime (65-75). Hat patterns rely on
  1/16T and 1/32 rolls.
- **One mono 808 as the bass.** Most modern trap 808s are pre-saturated
  samples that need only minimal processing. Resist plugin stacking.
- **Sidechain compression on the 808** keyed from the kick: fast attack
  (~1 ms), release ~80-150 ms (tuned to end just after the kick tail),
  2-4 dB GR. Lets the kick transient pop without ducking the 808 envelope.
- **Metro Boomin's alternative:** instead of compressor sidechain, *cut*
  the 808 MIDI so it only plays where the kick isn't. Eliminates low-end
  overlap entirely. (Michael Musco breakdown)
- **Multiband sidechain** (FabFilter Pro-MB) targeting only 60-150 Hz on the
  808 keeps harmonic upper region audible while kick fundamental cuts through.
- **Saturate the 808 mid-band (~150-400 Hz)** with a soft clipper, Decapitator,
  or iZotope Trash so the bass note translates on phones/earbuds.
- **Snare/clap layering: low body (150-250 Hz) + crack (2-5 kHz) + air
  (8-12 kHz).** Always check phase between layers; nudge or polarity-flip
  to fix hollowness.
- **Hi-hat panning:** open hat slightly opposite to closed hat (e.g. closed
  +10 R, open -10 L). Stereo width without breaking mono.
- **Vocal chain (Travis/Future style):** de-esser → 1176 fast comp 4-6 dB GR
  → LA-2A slow comp 2-3 dB → EQ (cut ~300 Hz mud, boost 5-8 kHz presence) →
  short plate ~1.2 s mixed -18 dB → 1/8 dotted slap. Keep dry and upfront.
- **Master chain order:** bus EQ → bus comp (1-2 dB GR) → soft clipper
  (1-2 dB shave) → limiter (final ceiling -1 dBTP). Trap targets -6 to -8
  LUFS integrated.
- **Kick/808 balance:** kick peaks ~2-3 dB above the 808; 808 sustain carries
  the body. Tune the kick two octaves above the 808 root.

### DON'T's

- **Don't layer two 808s in the same octave** — phase and mud guaranteed. If
  more weight is needed, layer a high-tuned kick on top, not another sub.
- **Don't EQ-cut blindly at 250-300 Hz on the 808** — that's where the body
  lives. Only cut if you actually hear mud.
- **Don't over-process pre-saturated commercial 808 samples.** Double-
  saturating destroys pitch.
- **Don't widen the 808 or kick.** Sub content must be mono.
- **Don't put long reverb on lead vocals.** Modern trap is dry vocal upfront;
  depth comes from short plate sends and slap, not hall. (Future is the rare
  exception.)
- **Don't crush the master with a limiter alone aiming for -6 LUFS.** Soft-
  clip first, then limit. Limiter-only = pumping and lost punch.
- **Don't put the snare on every 2 and 4 with no variation.** Use ghost
  snares, rolls, occasional half-bar absences.
- **Don't ignore phase between snare layers.** Two snares triggering
  simultaneously can hollow each other out — nudge by samples or flip polarity.
- **Don't overcompress the master before clipping.** ~1-2 dB GR max.
